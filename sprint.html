What the sprint is

Most people come to the sprint when they've rewritten the same thing so many times that changing the words isn't working anymore. The problem isn't the copy; it's the structure underneath it.

Most communications support starts at the output layer — the copy, the campaign, the content calendar. The sprint starts earlier.

At the question: what are you actually trying to say, and to whom, and why does it matter that they hear it this way?

That's not a writing question; it's a clarity question. And clarity is the work.

What you leave with

At the end of the sprint, you have a Message Architecture Brief: a working document that goes underneath all the content, campaigns, and communications you'll ever make.

It names:

  • Your audience's reality — who you're talking to, what they believe before they encounter you, and what needs to shift for the message to land
  • Your positioning — what you are, what you are not, and why that distinction matters to the people you're trying to reach
  • Your message hierarchy — what to lead with, what follows, and what supports it across different rooms and formats
  • Your language guardrails — phrases to use, phrases to retire, and how the message should sound when you're not in the room
  • Your application guidance — how to use the Brief across the homepage, decks, emails, vendor briefs, and presentations

It is the foundation before your content.

This document should change how you communicate for years. If it doesn't, the sprint wasn't done right.
