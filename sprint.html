What the sprint is

Most communications support starts at the output layer — the copy, the campaign, the content calendar. The sprint starts earlier.

At the question: what are you actually trying to say, and to whom, and why does it matter that they hear it this way?

That's not a writing question. It's a clarity question. And clarity is the work.

What you leave with

At the end of the sprint, you have a Message Architecture Brief — a working document that goes underneath all the content, campaigns, and communications you'll ever make. It names your audience's reality, your positioning, the message hierarchy, and the language guardrails that keep everything consistent across rooms and registers.

It is the foundation before your content.

This document should change how you communicate for years. If it doesn't, the sprint wasn't done right.

This sprint is for specific people.

Good fit

  • Messaging complexity is slowing progress or stalling decisions
  • Different audiences are hearing different versions of what you do
  • Language carries political, reputational, or organizational risk
  • You need a durable foundation — not a draft, not talking points
  • Decision-makers are present and ready to engage
  • You have a specific initiative, shift, or moment that requires clarity before you move
